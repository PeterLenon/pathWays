Problem Statement Mississippi consistently ranks among the highest in the United States for chronic health conditions, including diabetes, heart disease, and infant mortality. These issues are often treated as independent public health challenges; however, a closer examination reveals a shared systemic driver: limited income. Mississippi faces a significant diabetes burden, driven in part by limited access to affordable, nutritious food. Lower-income households are more likely to rely on inexpensive, low-nutrient diets due to cost constraints and the prevalence of food deserts. As of 2024, Mississippi’s poverty rate is approximately 17.8%, with Hinds County experiencing an even higher rate of 21.6%. These economic conditions directly influence dietary choices and long-term health outcomes. Similarly, chronic heart disease outcomes are worsened by delayed or avoided medical care. High costs associated with insurance, primary care visits, and prescription medication discourage early intervention. As a result, many individuals only seek treatment when conditions become severe, leading to higher mortality rates and increased healthcare burdens. Mississippi also has one of the highest infant mortality rates in the nation, with approximately 9.7 deaths per 1,000 live births compared to the national average of 5.5. Contributing factors include limited access to prenatal and pediatric care, inadequate nutrition, and shortages in trained healthcare personnel. These shortages are partially driven by barriers to higher education and professional training, particularly in fields such as nursing. While these challenges appear distinct, they converge on a common constraint: limited income restricts access to healthcare, education, and essential resources. This raises a critical question: how can individuals increase their earning potential in a way that is both realistic and informed? Data indicates that higher levels of education are strongly associated with increased earnings. In Hinds County, approximately 88.7% of residents aged 25 and older have a high school diploma, yet only 31.2% hold a bachelor’s degree or higher. Nationally, individuals with only a high school diploma earn significantly less than those with a bachelor’s degree, with median weekly earnings of approximately $946 compared to $1,533, respectively. However, the decision to pursue higher education or alternative career pathways is not straightforward. Several key barriers contribute to this gap: Financial Uncertainty – High tuition costs, fear of debt, and lost income during education create significant risk Information Gaps – Limited access to clear, localized data on which pathways lead to higher income Academic Preparedness – Many students are not adequately prepared for college-level coursework Social and Environmental Factors – Limited mentorship and exposure to viable career paths Psychological Barriers – Fear of failure and risk aversion influence decision-making Administrative Friction – Complex application and financial aid processes discourage follow-through These barriers reveal the underlying problem: individuals are required to make high-stakes career decisions under uncertainty, with incomplete information and constrained resources. To address this challenge, we propose an AI-powered Job Path Optimizer. This system evaluates multiple career pathways—including college degrees, vocational training, certifications, and direct workforce entry—and provides personalized recommendations based on an individual’s background, financial constraints, and local labor market conditions. By integrating job market data, wage statistics, and skill requirements, the system predicts potential income outcomes and identifies the most effective path for economic mobility. Rather than promoting a single solution, the system enables individuals to make informed, data-driven decisions that balance cost, risk, and long-term benefit.
